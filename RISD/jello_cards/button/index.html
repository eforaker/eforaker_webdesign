In her revolutionary book, The Feminine Mystique, Betty Friedan described “the problem that has no name” as the unhappiness and inability to live up to the feminine mystique. This problem was amplified by advertising. Ads depicted beautiful, smiling women in the kitchen, happy to be the domestic caregiver. Midcentury food trends are inherently linked to femininity, a notable example being jello salad.

The appeal of jello salad was that it is salad completely in control of itself. Taste was unimportant as long as it looked beautiful. The introduction of instant jello should have meant less domestic labor, but society pressured women to add more work into the cooking process, so as not to be perceived as a lazy wife or uncaring mother. Elaborate jello dishes were a physical representation of the pressure to perform femininity.

Jello salads have mostly fallen out of favor (except in some Midwestern states), but performing femininity has not. One means of performing femininity is nail polish. From a young age, my mother stressed the importance of having “presentable” hands and unchipped nail polish. The names of nail polish colors are amusing and absurd, but they reflect society’s impossible expectations of femininity. Women should be hyper-sexual yet innocent. Perfect yet carefree. Independent yet desirable to men.
